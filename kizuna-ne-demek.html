What does Kizuna mean?

July 20, 2026 · Kizuna
絆
kizuna

Kizuna (絆) means "bond" in Japanese. Not a casual connection, not a simple friendship — but the deep, unbreakable ties between people: family, lifelong friends, communities that hold each other up. It is one of those words that every Japanese person feels instantly, yet no dictionary fully captures.

From a rope to a bond: the origin of the word

The story of kizuna begins in a surprisingly humble place. Originally, the word described the rope used to tether horses, dogs, and other animals. The kanji 絆 itself is built from 糸, the radical for "thread", and linguists trace the "-zuna" part to 綱 (tsuna), meaning "rope".

The word has existed for over a thousand years — it appears in a Japanese dictionary compiled in the 10th century. By the 12th century, in the epic "The Tale of the Heike", kizuna was already being used as a metaphor for the loving bond between parents and their children. A word that once meant a physical tether had become a word for the invisible ties of the heart.

2011: The kanji that united a nation

On March 11, 2011, a devastating earthquake and tsunami struck northeastern Japan. In the months that followed, the entire country came together — neighbours sheltering neighbours, strangers helping strangers, and support pouring in from around the world.

At the end of that year, the Japanese public chose 絆 as the Kanji of the Year. It was the word that best described what had carried the nation through its darkest hour: the bonds between people. Since then, kizuna has carried an even deeper resonance in Japan — a symbol of solidarity, resilience, and mutual care.

Did you know?

Every December, the Japan Kanji Aptitude Testing Foundation announces the "Kanji of the Year" at Kiyomizu-dera temple in Kyoto, where the head priest paints the chosen character with a giant brush. In 2011, that character was 絆 — kizuna.
